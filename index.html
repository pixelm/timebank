The Mom Time Bank.

A small experiment in sharing the invisible work of family life.

Most moms spend hours every week doing things that aren't particularly difficult — but still take time, attention, and mental energy.

  • Researching camps.
  • Finding contractors.
  • Comparing insurance options.
  • Scheduling appointments.
  • Planning trips.
  • Following up on emails.
  • Coordinating the hundreds of tiny details that make family life run.

The work is often invisible. And it adds up.

⁂

I · The ExperimentA small pilot, ten moms, one short week.

I'm gathering ten moms for a simple pilot, running through June 12th.

Each participant contributes one hour of focused help to another mom. In return, she can ask for one hour of help on a life-admin task that's been sitting on her mental to-do list.

No money changes hands. We exchange time, attention, and support.

To keep things moving, every request should fit in roughly one hour — and once a task is claimed, it's completed within five days.

⁂

II · ExamplesThe kinds of things a volunteer might do.

  • Research summer camps
  • Find and compare contractors
  • Call service providers for quotes
  • Create a vacation shortlist
  • Plan a birthday party
  • Research healthcare providers
  • Build a spreadsheet of options
  • Organize information and recommendations
  • Draft emails and communications

The goal isn't perfection. The goal is to lighten the mental load.

⁂

III · How It WorksFive small steps.

  1. Fill out a short request form describing a task that could reasonably be completed in about one hour.
  2. Your request is added to a shared task board.
  3. Another mom volunteers to take your task.
  4. You choose a task from the board and help another mom.
  5. Connect directly via WhatsApp and complete the task within five days.
⁂

IV · The Anatomy of a Good RequestWhat works, what doesn't.

Good requests are:

  • Specific
  • Actionable
  • Mostly remote
  • About an hour

For example:

Find three nature-based summer camps for a six-year-old within a 15-minute drive of my home in South Austin that still have available spots, cost between $300–$500 per week, and include links, dates, and registration information.
Call local deck contractors and gather pricing and earliest availability for a 300 sq ft deck.
⁂

V · A Few Things This Isn'tTo keep the experiment honest.

  • Childcare exchange
  • In-person labor
  • Professional consulting
  • Ongoing commitments

This is a virtual exchange of time and attention.

⁂

VI · Why Another MomAnd not a virtual assistant.

You could hire a VA to do many of these tasks. But another mom brings something different.

She already understands the context and cares about what you need. She can put herself in your shoes. You spend less time explaining and more time getting help.

One of the challenges with outsourcing small life-admin tasks is that describing the task can feel almost as exhausting as doing it yourself. Another mom needs fewer instructions — she's navigating many of the same challenges.

And beyond the practical help, there's something else. When a mom helps another mom, she's not just completing a task. She's sharing the load.

The goal isn't simply efficiency. It's creating a little more ease, care, and breathing room for one another.

⁂

I've realized that some of the most draining parts of parenting aren't the big things. They're the endless small things.

  • The tabs left open in your browser.
  • The phone call you haven't made.
  • The research project that keeps getting postponed.
  • The decision that's been sitting in your head for weeks.

I'm curious what would happen if we shared that load.

Maybe nothing. Maybe we all get a few hours back. Maybe we discover a new way of supporting one another.

There's only one way to find out.
